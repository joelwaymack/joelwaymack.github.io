I was listening in on a meeting recently and one of my coworkers said, "Let me see if I can find the correct resource for this request." Was he talking about finding the location of a PowerPoint presentation? Nope. Was he talking about researching and choosing the right technology? Nope. He was talking about identifying the right person to assist a customer. Let me say this from the outset: **people aren't resources**.

# Word Communication
Let's start off with a simple premise: words matter. I don't want to jump too far down the rabbit hole of communication and language theory, so suffice it to say that the words we use communicate ideas based on shared symbols. Those symbols, our words, require a common understanding of the shared symbol set to make communication effective.

For example, when I talk to you about a "dog" I recently saw, we both have a shared understanding that the word "dog" refers to a 4 legged, furry canine that was alive. Our shared understanding of the word "dog" allows communication to effectively occur. If I said "I recently saw a dog" but start talking to you about the short, 10lb creature with pointy ears, sharp teeth, long claws, and general attitude of "I don't care about anything" you'd probably stop me and clarify that I'm talking about a dog and not a cat. The word I use is crucial in our ability to communicate.

> "Good words are worth much, and cost little."
—George Herbert

Hopefully it makes sense that words matter. When I use a word, I want to be accurate so that I communicate the correct concept to help, not hinder, our shared understanding.

This shared understanding is crucial when we discuss that **people aren't resources**.

# What is a Resource
Let's start off by talking about what a resouce is. When I think of a resource, my mind is immediately drawn to the board game Settlers of Catan. In that game you use resources (sheep, wood, brick, ore, and wheat) to build improvements and gain victory points.

Beyond games, when I think of resources, I think of money, time, natural commodities, and the like. If we're talking about a shared understanding for effective communication, then it seems like people don't really fit into this set of things very well. But, my knowledge of English could also be lacking so let's go to an authoritative source.

Dictionary.com defines a resource as "a source of supply, support, or aid, especially one that can be readily drawn upon when needed." It seems like people might fall into this definition if there wasn't one
